Restricted Dog Breeds in Ireland

Irish legislation created the classification context, while its limits make clear why image classification cannot determine legal identity or dangerousness.

1998

Additional controls for specified dogs

The Control of Dogs Regulations 1998 apply additional public-place controls to listed breeds, types and their crosses. These include secure muzzling and a sufficiently strong chain or leash no longer than two metres, held by someone over sixteen.

Read the 1998 Regulations ↗
The dataset

Six represented restricted breeds

The Stanford Dogs dataset contained six breeds used for the restricted class: Bull Mastiff, Doberman Pinscher, German Shepherd, Rhodesian Ridgeback, Rottweiler and Staffordshire Bull Terrier. Twenty-four other breeds formed the comparison class.

This is a reproducible research construction—not a complete representation of every dog covered by Irish law.

The boundary

Image classification is not legal identification

Breed labels can be uncertain, crosses are difficult to determine visually, appearance does not establish behaviour, and a model trained on curated images does not reproduce real deployment conditions.

The experiment benchmarks CNN transfer learning. It does not decide whether an individual dog is legally restricted or dangerous.
Later policy development

XL Bully regulations created a separate, stricter regime.

The 2024 regulations define an XL Bully using a physical-conformation standard. Since 1 February 2025, ownership requires a valid Certificate of Exemption. This later development reinforces the project’s central caution: legal classification can depend on rules and evidence that an image classifier cannot supply.

02 · The catalyst

ImageNet changed what computer vision could become.

The breakthrough was not merely a new neural network. It was the combination of a huge labelled dataset, a demanding competition and enough compute to train deep models at scale.

1

Before ImageNet

Vision systems often depended heavily on engineered features. Researchers designed descriptors first, then trained a classifier on top.

Image→Hand-crafted features→Classifier
2

ImageNet + ILSVRC

A very large labelled image collection created a common arena where models could be compared on the same recognition task.

1,000classes in the classic ILSVRC classification task
3

What changed

Instead of manually defining the useful visual features, deep networks could learn increasingly abstract representations directly from images.

Pixels→Edges→Textures→Objects
The key ideaImageNet supplied the scale. CNNs supplied the representation learning. GPUs supplied the practical compute.

03 · CNN mechanics

How does a CNN actually see an image?

A CNN does not “see a dog” in one step. It repeatedly applies local learned filters and builds more useful feature representations layer by layer.

×
-101-101-101
→
Feature mapstrong response where the learned pattern is present
Convolution

Learn a local detector

A small kernel slides across the image. The same weights are reused everywhere, making CNNs efficient and naturally suited to spatial patterns.

Activation

Keep useful signals

Non-linear activations let stacked layers model far more complex patterns than a sequence of linear operations could.

Pooling / stride

Compress spatial detail

Later representations become smaller while retaining important signals, increasing the effective receptive field.

Hierarchy

Build features on features

Early layers respond to simple structure; deeper layers combine those responses into textures, parts and higher-level concepts.

04 · Architecture evolution

Every famous CNN solved a problem left by the previous generation.

Do not memorise architecture names. Memorise the design pressure that produced each one.

2012

AlexNet

Can deep CNNs win at scale?

GPU training, ReLU, dropout and data augmentation helped produce the ImageNet breakthrough.

2014

VGG

Can depth be made systematic?

Repeated small 3×3 convolutions created a clean, uniform architecture and showed the power of going deeper.

2014–15

Inception

Which spatial scale matters?

Parallel branches process information at several receptive-field sizes, combining them efficiently.

2015

ResNet

Why does deeper training degrade?

Skip connections let layers learn residual changes around an identity pathway, making much deeper networks trainable.

2016

Xception

Can convolution be factorised?

Depthwise-separable convolution separates spatial filtering from channel mixing, improving parameter efficiency.

2016

InceptionResNetV2

Can two strong ideas be combined?

Multi-scale Inception modules gain residual shortcuts, blending representational richness with easier optimisation.

2017

NASNet

Can machines search for architectures?

Neural architecture search shifts part of network design from hand-crafted engineering toward automated optimisation.

2020

Vision Transformer

Do images require convolution?

An image can be split into patches and processed as a sequence using Transformer self-attention.

What is a fully connected or dense layer?

Dense and fully connected mean the same thing: every input arriving at the layer has a learned weight connecting it to every neuron in that layer. In this project the final dense layer had just one sigmoid neuron. It learned how strongly each pooled CNN feature should count for or against the restricted class.

The dense layer did not inspect raw pixels. The CNN base had already transformed the image into meaningful feature signals.

Fine-tuning

The winning benchmark model, InceptionResNetV2, shown before and after its final 20 base layers were fine-tuned.

TRAINING HISTORY

How to interpret these plots →
1
START FROM THE SELECTED MODEL

Load its best checkpoint

InceptionResNetV2 was selected from the six-model benchmark. Before fine-tuning, its ImageNet CNN base was frozen and only its classification head had learned this task.

2
OPEN THE DEEPEST PART

Unfreeze the final 20 base layers

Earlier layers stayed fixed. Only the deepest 20 layers of the pretrained base—plus the classification head—were allowed to change.

3
PROPAGATE THE ERROR BACKWARDS

Backpropagation calculates useful changes

Each prediction produced a loss. Gradients carried that error backwards from the sigmoid output, through the head and into the 20 unfrozen layers.

4
MAKE SMALL UPDATES

Adam updates trainable weights at 1e-5

The low learning rate made cautious adjustments, helping deeper features specialise without rapidly overwriting useful ImageNet knowledge.

5
CONTROL GENERALISATION

Validate, stop and retain the best checkpoint

Training allowed up to 15 epochs with class weighting and patience 4. Early stopping limited overfitting, and the best validation checkpoint—not simply the last epoch—was evaluated.

What “before fine-tuning” means:the selected model’s pretrained CNN base was still frozen, while its new classification head had been trained for restricted-versus-unrestricted prediction. During fine-tuning, the final 20 base layers were made trainable together and backpropagation updated that trainable region.

10 · Before and beyond CNNs

What came before CNNs—and what followed them?

Before deep CNNs, computer-vision systems relied heavily on hand-designed features and separate classifiers. CNNs learned useful features directly from images. Transformers later relaxed CNN assumptions and learned relationships between image patches through attention.

CNN

Locality is built in.

Pixels→Local filters→Feature hierarchy→Prediction

Excellent inductive bias for images; efficient learning of local spatial structure.

VS
ViT

Relationships are learned.

The important historical point: ViT did not prove that convolution was “wrong”. It showed that, given enough data and compute, a less image-specific architecture could become extremely competitive in vision.
