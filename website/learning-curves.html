Plain-language training guide

What is a learning curve telling me?

A learning curve is the model’s training story. It shows whether performance is improving, whether learning transfers to unseen validation images, and when further training may stop helping.

START WITH THE X-AXIS

One epoch = one complete pass through the training data.

At the end of each epoch, the graph records training and validation performance. Reading from left to right shows how learning changes over time.

01 · The four signals

Accuracy asks “how often correct?” Loss asks “how wrong?”

Training values come from images used to update the model. Validation values come from a separate set used to check generalisation during training; validation images do not update the weights.

Training accuracy

How often is the model correct on training batches?

It should generally rise. Augmentation, dropout and other training-only effects can make this lower or noisier than validation accuracy.

Validation accuracy

How often is the model correct on unseen validation images?

It should rise with training accuracy and then level off. It is a better guide to generalisation than training accuracy alone.

Training loss

How large is the model’s optimisation penalty on training batches?

It should generally fall. Loss uses confidence as well as correctness, so two models with the same accuracy can have different loss.

Validation loss

How large is that penalty on unseen validation images?

It should fall and stabilise. If it turns upward while training loss keeps falling, overfitting may be starting.

Do not confuse themAccuracy is a proportion of correct labels. Loss is the function the optimiser tries to minimise. Lower loss is better, but loss is not simply “one minus accuracy”.

02 · A reading routine

Read every learning curve in four passes.

Do not begin by judging whether one line is “high”. First examine direction, separation, turning points and stability.

  1. 1

    Direction

    Are both accuracy curves generally rising? Are both loss curves generally falling? If yes, useful learning is taking place.

  2. 2

    Training–validation gap

    Are the paired curves close? A widening gap can mean the model is learning the training data better than it generalises.

  3. 3

    Turning point

    Where does validation performance stop improving? The best checkpoint is often near the lowest validation loss, not automatically the final epoch.

  4. 4

    Stability

    Small fluctuations are normal. Large repeated jumps can indicate a noisy validation set, an aggressive learning rate or unstable optimisation.

Can validation accuracy be higher than training accuracy?

Yes. Training may be deliberately harder because it uses augmented images and training-time regularisation such as dropout. Validation uses clean images with dropout disabled. A modest validation advantage is therefore not, by itself, evidence of data leakage.

03 · Recognising patterns

What should make me look more closely?

These are diagnostic clues, not automatic verdicts. Always check the data pipeline, class balance and final held-out test results as well.

Healthy convergence

Both datasets improve together

Accuracy rises, loss falls, and training and validation remain reasonably close before settling.

Possible overfitting

Training improves while validation deteriorates

Training loss keeps falling, but validation loss rises or validation accuracy falls. The generalisation gap widens.

Possible underfitting

Both performances plateau too low

Training and validation may be close, but neither becomes strong. The model, features or training setup may not capture enough of the task.

Possible instability

Validation jumps sharply between epochs

Investigate learning rate, validation-set size, class composition and whether evaluation is deterministic.
