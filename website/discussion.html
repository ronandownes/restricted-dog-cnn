What the benchmark, fine-tuning experiment, learning curves and Grad-CAM cases mean when considered together.

Benchmark finding

All models used the same binary task and evaluation logic, allowing their frozen transfer-learning performance to be compared directly.

InceptionResNetV2 provided the strongest overall balance

Its combination of accuracy, precision, recall and F1 made it the selected model for fine-tuning. Selection did not depend on accuracy alone.

Fine-tuning changed the error trade-off

Unfreezing the final 20 layers improved accuracy, precision and F1, while restricted-class recall fell slightly.

Fewer false positives, two more false negatives

False positives fell from 28 to 14, while false negatives rose from 5 to 7. Fine-tuning improved overall balance, but it did not improve every consequence of error.

Learning curves explain the training process

Training and validation accuracy and loss show how learning developed across epochs. They complement the final held-out test metrics rather than replacing them.

Best checkpoint matters more than the last epoch

Validation monitoring and early stopping were used to retain the strongest checkpoint and reduce unnecessary training after improvement had stalled.

Grad-CAM added evidence, not proof

The selected cases show where class-relevant deep activations occurred across correct and incorrect predictions.

Attention to the dog is reassuring; background reliance is a warning

The heatmaps help identify whether the model attended to plausible dog regions or potentially relied on contextual shortcuts. A plausible heatmap does not establish causal or human-like reasoning.

Limitations and conclusion

The results are strong for the constructed Stanford Dogs experiment, but the task remains narrower than real-world breed identification.

Constructed classes

Six represented restricted breeds were grouped against a sampled set of comparison breeds. This is not the full legal category.

Dataset conditions

Curated images may contain breed, pose and background patterns that differ from photographs encountered in practice.

Visual explanation

Grad-CAM can expose possible shortcuts, but it cannot prove why a prediction occurred or eliminate bias.

Overall conclusion

ImageNet transfer learning performed strongly, InceptionResNetV2 ranked first, and fine-tuning improved overall test balance. The experiment demonstrates a reproducible computer-vision workflow while also showing why headline accuracy must be interpreted alongside error counts, training behaviour and explanation evidence.
