\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

This dissertation investigates whether transfer learning with
convolutional neural networks can support the visual
classification of dog breeds subject to additional controls
under Irish law.

A binary restricted/unrestricted image-classification task was
constructed from the Stanford Dogs dataset using six represented
restricted breeds and a reproducibly selected sample of
twenty-four unrestricted breeds. Six ImageNet-pretrained
architectures were evaluated as frozen feature extractors under
a common pipeline. The models compared were VGG16, ResNet50,
InceptionV3, Xception, InceptionResNetV2 and NASNetMobile.

InceptionResNetV2 provided the strongest frozen result, achieving
97.08\% accuracy, 87.38\% precision, 98.42\% recall and an F1
score of 92.57\%. Fine-tuning the final twenty layers improved
accuracy to 98.25\%, precision to 93.88\% and F1 to 95.34\%,
while recall reduced slightly to 96.84\%. The corresponding
confusion matrix contained 826 true negatives, 12 false
positives, 6 false negatives and 184 true positives.

The findings therefore demonstrate that fine-tuning changed the
operating trade-off rather than uniformly improving every
metric: false positives were more than halved, but false
negatives increased from three to six.

A qualitative Grad-CAM study of twelve fixed cases was used to
examine true-positive, true-negative, false-positive and
false-negative decisions. The maps frequently overlapped the
dog, but some predictions were influenced by pose, scale,
contrast and background structure.

The study concludes that transfer learning is effective for this
controlled dataset, while emphasising that a photograph-only
classifier cannot establish legal breed identity and should not
be treated as an autonomous enforcement mechanism. The main
contribution is a reproducible comparison of established CNN
architectures, a metric-aware fine-tuning analysis, and a
critical account of the technical, legal and ethical limits of
image-based restricted-breed classification.
